DEVELOPMENT Deployed Site

00 Intro

The Development assignment required students to design and implement a fully usable aggregator using React, which included features for filtering and sorting elements on a page and adding them to a subsection of the screen devoted to organizing "favorites" or a "cart." By performing a competitive analysis at the start of the assignment, designs were based off of the perceived strengths of three different designs so they could take advantage of how effective aggregators have been implemented in industry.

01 Competitive Analysis

For my three example websites, I chose Spotify, Pinterest, and Amazon. Although the three sites had a lot of features in common (like user-created lists, different view specs, and searching within lists), their designs differed slightly within how they balanced master lists and user-created lists.

01 Spotify

Given their main function of curating music for users, Spotify had more advantages for filtering and saving favorites locally by revolving around user-created folders. However, Spotify lacked a "master list" for all saved songs unlike the other two sites, which could be helpful when users look for songs that they saved but might not remember the location of.

In terms of its design, Spotify devotes most of the screen to playlists and songs in a given playlist. By simplifying each action into an icon that can display on hover for favoriting songs and using similarity to group similar actions together (such as the “favorite” icon in multiple locations wherever a song appears), Spotify highlights the main actions it wants its users to take without being too cluttered.

02 Pinterest

Like Spotify, Pinterest had user-created lists and one-click saving, but it also had an unorganized master list, which made it easier to see all saves in one location, and its view specs differed in the level of detail displayed rather than page layout. However, Pinterest didn’t have any way to filter content by media type or topic.

Pinterest’s design sits between Spotify and Amazon’s in terms of complexity with both a tree layout and one-click saves. This allows for Pinterest to display the most pertinent actions while adding complexity when commenting or writing notes on pins. Instead of showing both lists and items, Pinterest spotlights the items themselves, relying on similarity and repetition to unify the page; this also allows users to focus on the pins, with boards being secondary.

03 Amazon

With master lists, user-created lists, and filtering, Amazon makes it easy to find saved items. Furthermore, its qualitative customer ratings also add to its credibility compared to Pinterest and Spotify, which use more quantitative metrics. However, Amazon does not support one-click saves or saving favorites locally.

Amazon’s design is more robust than the other two sites; buttons for saving or adding items to one’s cart sit beside the info, highlights the products themselves. With grouping, Amazon makes information easy to peruse while creating a nuanced experience with more visible options. However, by using similarity within text links versus various kinds of buttons, Amazon allows users to do more without compromising visual clarity.

02 Competitive Analysis Reflection

Given that having a master list, one-click save, and view specs were three features that were not universal across the aggregators, yet still features that seemed to be beneficial for the user experience, I decided to implement these three functions as part of my website.

Although other features like user-created lists and filtering within lists were also helpful, given the scope of the assignment to encompass smaller numbers of items, I felt that it didn't make much sense to implement user-created lists and filtering within lists given the small number of items.

Starting with lofi mockups, I sketched some different screens for how the user journey might look as they navigate through the site. Taking careful consideration with the interfacing of different menus, I also played around with page layout and how it might be affected by the opening and closing of different compartments on the page.

After some revision, I took the lofi mockups into a higher fidelity, adding color and font to determine the visual style of the pages. The designs changed throughout the development process as I discovered holes in my user flow and changed the designs to match potential user actions, but the core of the visual identity and experience stayed relatively consistent.

03 Functionality

My final website is an interface detailing various museums in New York that I felt were relevant for cultural (art) education and interesting places to visit while in the area. The two buttons in the top left corner allow for viewing two different aggregators, one for a master list (favorites), and another for a more specific list (save for later).

Within these lists, users have the ability to switch between grid and list views of the data given the limited real estate on the screen. Using a more typical convention, menus can be closed by both clicking on the button that opened them, and also by clicking the "Close" button in the bottom of the menu. The three menus employ similar design principles so they can be grouped visually and experienced similarly in terms of the visual language, actions, and motion implemented.

Within each card displayed on the page, users have the ability to view information and visit links to the museum sites upon hovering, and they are also able to add and remove elements from both lists using the corresponding buttons on the cards. A filter menu, opened via the icon in the bottom left, allows for users to simultaneously sort by museum ticket price and filter based on area and genre. The number of results, like the number of favorited or saved items, is displayed in the filter menu for consistency and to show when there may be no results returned. All of the menus have the ability to clear filters or aggregated items.

04 Conclusions

Overall, even though it was a difficult process to navigate through the different hooks while keeping track of all the moving parts of my application, I thought it was an informative way of translating market research into stronger products. Additionally, finding the most visually appealing way of displaying data while creating a fluid and satisfying experience is an ongoing challenge, but being able to create a usable prototype was a fulfilling way to learn how translating designs into code could be applied to real-life projects.
